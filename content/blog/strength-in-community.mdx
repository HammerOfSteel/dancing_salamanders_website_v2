I am a member of the Sad Dads Club.

Not metaphorically. There is an actual club — a community of fathers who have lost children, who have gathered to say to each other: *I know. I see you. You are not alone in this.*

Joining was one of the harder things I have done. Not because the people were unwelcoming — they were, from the first moment, warm beyond anything I expected. But because walking through the door meant admitting something I was still trying not to admit. That I was this. A sad dad. That it was real and it was mine and it was not going away.

## Why Grief Isolates

There is a particular kind of loneliness that grief creates. It is not simply the loneliness of being without the person you lost — though that is enormous and relentless. It is the loneliness of being among people who do not know what to do with your grief. Who are uncomfortable with it. Who want to help but say the wrong things, or say nothing at all.

Grief makes people around you awkward. They are afraid — of saying the wrong thing, of reminding you, of making it worse. And so they often say very little. Or they change the subject. Or they ask "how are you?" in the way that makes it clear that "fine" is the only acceptable answer.

And so the grieving person learns to contain it. To perform adequacy. To answer "fine" when they mean something entirely different. To make the people around them comfortable by making their grief invisible.

This is exhausting. And it deepens the isolation.

## What Community Does

When you are with others who have experienced the same loss, none of that performance is necessary. The thing that made everyone else uncomfortable is the common ground here. You can say what you actually mean. You can be in whatever state you are in that day — and it is met not with discomfort but with recognition.

There is a phrase that gets used in bereavement circles: *you don't have to explain yourself here*. I did not understand how much I needed that until I experienced it.

Community does something else too. It shows you survival. When you are in the deep acute phase of grief, you cannot imagine that it will ever be anything other than what it is right now. The people who are six months, a year, five years ahead of you are not proof that it disappears — it doesn't — but they are proof that it can be carried. That life can be rebuilt around it. That there is a way through, even if it is not the way you expected.

## The Sad Dads Club

The [Sad Dads Club](https://saddadsclub.org/) grew from exactly this need. Fathers grieving the deaths of children, finding each other and recognising that the particular experience of bereaved fatherhood — often underserved by services, often expected to be the stoic support for others in the family, often isolated — needed its own space.

Grief is not gendered in the sense that fathers do not grieve differently than mothers in kind. But there are ways that social expectations shape how grief is expressed and supported. Fathers are often expected to hold it together, to be the strong one, to process privately. The space to simply be undone — to not hold it together — can be harder to find.

The Sad Dads Club is that space. And it has been, for me, one of the most important things.

## On Asking for Help

I did not ask for help easily. I suspect few bereaved parents do. There is something about grief — particularly the grief of losing a child — that can carry a false sense that seeking support is a betrayal. That to need help is to not be strong enough. That the only proper response to the enormity of the loss is to carry it alone.

This is not true. Asking for help is not weakness. It is the opposite — it is the courage to admit what is actually happening and to take a step toward not being destroyed by it.

If you are a bereaved parent reading this: you do not have to manage this alone. There are people who understand. There are communities that will hold you without needing you to perform being fine.

You are allowed to reach for them.
