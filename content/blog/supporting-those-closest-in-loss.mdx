When someone we love is grieving, the hardest thing to sit with is our own helplessness. We want to do something. We want to fix it. We want to say the thing that makes it better.

There is no thing that makes it better. But there is something we can do: we can ask. We can listen. We can stay.

Below are 75 questions you might ask someone who is grieving. They are not a script — they are possibilities, invitations. Choose what feels appropriate for your relationship. The goal is not to run through a list but to communicate: *I am here, I want to understand, I am not afraid of this.*

## Types of Grief

Before the questions, a brief note on the many shapes grief takes. Grief is not only the grief of death. There is:

- **Bereavement grief** — the loss of someone who has died
- **Anticipatory grief** — the grief that begins before the death, when a loss is coming
- **Ambiguous loss** — loss without a clear ending (dementia, estrangement, missing persons)
- **Disenfranchised grief** — grief that is not socially acknowledged (miscarriage, the loss of a pet, the death of someone the griever was not "supposed" to love)
- **Cumulative grief** — multiple losses, one following another, without time to recover
- **Complicated grief** — grief that does not follow expected patterns and may involve prolonged impairment

Each of these has its own texture and its own particular invisibilities. Some forms of grief are more readily recognised and supported than others. If someone you love is experiencing grief that doesn't fit the "standard" category, they may be particularly isolated — and particularly in need of acknowledgement.

## Myths About Grief

A few common misconceptions worth naming:

**Myth: Time heals all wounds.**
Time changes grief, but it does not erase it. A bereaved parent does not stop grieving because enough years have passed. They learn — slowly, painfully — to carry the grief alongside life. This is not healing in the sense of ending.

**Myth: There are stages, and you progress through them.**
Kübler-Ross's stages were never intended as a linear progression. They are descriptions of emotional states that the dying and the bereaved can experience — not a ladder with a top. Grief is not a process with a finish line.

**Myth: Crying means you're not coping; not crying means you are.**
Both are normal. Grief manifests in countless ways. Someone who seems "strong" or "coping well" may be deeply submerged. Someone who cries often is processing, not failing to cope.

**Myth: Moving on means forgetting.**
There is a difference between moving on (leaving behind) and moving forward (carrying forward). The goal of grief is not to forget the person you lost but to find a way to continue living while carrying them with you.

## 75 Questions to Ask Someone Who Is Grieving

### About Their Loved One

1. What was [name] like?
2. What's one of your favourite memories of them?
3. What did they love most in the world?
4. What did they smell like? Sound like?
5. What made them laugh?
6. What did they care deeply about?
7. What do you wish more people knew about them?
8. What was their relationship with you like — what made it special?
9. What habits did they have that you can still picture?
10. Is there something they said that you find yourself returning to?

### About the Person Grieving

11. How are you — really?
12. What's the hardest part of today?
13. What does it feel like, right now, in your body?
14. Are you sleeping?
15. Are you eating?
16. When did you last feel okay?
17. Is there anything you're afraid to feel?
18. Are there moments when you forget — and then remember again?
19. What does a hard day look like for you?
20. What does a less hard day look like?
21. Are there things that are helping, even a little?
22. Is there anything that's making it harder?
23. What do you wish people understood about what you're going through?
24. Are people saying things that are hurting, even if they don't mean to?
25. Is there something you're carrying that you haven't told anyone?

### About Practical Support

26. Do you need company, or do you need space right now?
27. Is there anything I can do today?
28. Can I bring you food this week?
29. Are there things that need doing that you don't have capacity for?
30. Is there someone I can call on your behalf?
31. Do you need help with anything official — paperwork, calls, arrangements?
32. Is there anyone else who's supporting you?
33. Are you connected to any community or support groups?
34. Do you need help finding support?
35. Is there anything you've been putting off that you want help with?

### About Remembering

36. How do you want to mark important dates?
37. Is there anything you do to feel close to them?
38. Are there places that feel particularly significant?
39. What would it mean to you for others to remember them?
40. Is there a way I can help keep their memory alive?
41. Do you have things of theirs that matter to you?
42. Is there a ritual you've found helpful?
43. Is there anything you want to do in their memory?
44. What do you want people to know about how to remember them?
45. Is there something about them that you're afraid will be forgotten?

### About the Future

46. What does the future look like from where you are?
47. Is there anything you're looking forward to — even a small thing?
48. What would it mean to you to find a way forward?
49. What do you want your life to hold, going forward?
50. Is there anything you've learned about yourself through this?
51. Are there things you want to do that you've been putting off?
52. What would help you feel less alone in this?
53. What do you need from the people around you going forward?
54. Is there anything you're doing to take care of yourself?
55. Is there anything you've let go of that you needed to let go of?

### About Connection

56. Is there anyone you feel truly understood by?
57. Is there anyone who knew them that you can talk with?
58. Are there people you feel you can call when things are hard?
59. Is there someone who makes you feel less alone in this?
60. Are there people who are avoiding you because they don't know what to say?
61. Is loneliness part of what you're experiencing?
62. Is there a community you feel part of — even loosely?
63. Are there other people who loved them that you've connected with?
64. What does good company look like for you right now?
65. Is there a place where you feel safe to be exactly where you are?

### The Deeper Questions

66. Is there anything you wish you had said or done?
67. Is there anything you are at peace with?
68. What do you believe happens after death?
69. Is there anything that feels sacred about this grief?
70. Is there something in your life that has changed because of this loss?
71. What has surprised you about grief?
72. Is there anything you've found unexpectedly beautiful in the middle of this?
73. What do you want to hold onto from who they were?
74. Is there something this loss has taught you about love?
75. What would it mean to honour them with your life going forward?

---

You will not ask all of these. You will not ask most of them in any single conversation. But they are here as a reminder: there is always something more to ask, some way to go a little deeper, some invitation to open the door a little wider.

The grief is there whether you acknowledge it or not. When you acknowledge it — when you ask, when you listen, when you stay — you are doing something that matters more than most things.

You are not letting them be alone in it.
