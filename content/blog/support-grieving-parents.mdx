When a parent loses a child, the world shifts for everyone who loves them. Friends, family, colleagues — people who care and want to help — often find themselves paralysed. They are afraid of saying the wrong thing. They worry that mentioning the child will make things worse. They disappear, because they do not know what else to do.

The disappearing is usually the worst thing.

This post is for the people who want to stay. Who want to be present, even imperfectly. It is not a set of rules — grief does not follow rules — but a set of honest guides based on experience and the accounts of many bereaved parents.

## What Bereaved Parents Often Need

Before the list of dos and don'ts, some context.

A bereaved parent has experienced a loss that is, in many ways, a reversal of the natural order. Children are not supposed to die before their parents. The loss disrupts not just the present but the imagined future — a future that was, in many ways, the centre of their life.

This grief does not end. It changes. It reshapes. But a parent who has lost a child will carry that child with them for the rest of their life. There is no "getting over it." There is only learning to live alongside it.

What most bereaved parents want, more than anything, is to have their child acknowledged. To have their child's name spoken. To have someone understand that their child was a real person, a whole person, someone loved — not a sad event to be carefully avoided in conversation.

## What Helps

**Say the child's name.** This is perhaps the single most important thing. Parents are often surrounded by people who never mention the child's name again after the initial period of acute grief. But they think about their child every day. Hearing the name spoken by others is a profound comfort — proof that others remember, that the child's existence is acknowledged.

**Ask about the child.** "What was she like?" "Do you have a favourite memory of him?" These questions are not dangerous. They are gifts. They give the parent permission to talk about their child as the person they were.

**Show up, practically.** After a death, the first weeks are often full of people. Then they drift away — just as the acute shock is wearing off and the long, hard work of grief is beginning. Showing up six months later, a year later — with food, with an offer of company, with a simple "I've been thinking of you" — means more than many people realise.

**Check in on difficult dates.** The child's birthday, the anniversary of the death, Christmas, Mother's Day, Father's Day. These days are often excruciating for bereaved parents. A simple message — "I'm thinking of [name] today, and of you" — matters enormously.

**Be comfortable with silence and with tears.** You do not need to fix it or cheer them up. You just need to be there. Silence that is witnessed is different from silence that is alone.

**Follow their lead.** Some parents want to talk about their child constantly. Others find it too hard on some days. Let them set the pace. Ask if you're unsure.

## What Doesn't Help

**Avoiding the subject entirely.** This communicates to the parent that their child is too uncomfortable to mention — and, by extension, that their grief is a burden. It is profoundly isolating.

**Comparisons and minimisations.** "At least you have other children." "At least she didn't suffer." "At least you had him for the years you did." These statements, however well-meant, dismiss the specific loss — this child, this irreplaceable person. There is no "at least" that helps.

**Timelines and expectations.** "You need to move on." "It's been two years." "Wouldn't she want you to be happy?" Grief does not have a schedule. These statements add shame and pressure to what is already an unbearable weight.

**Making their grief about you.** "I can't imagine what you're going through — it would destroy me." This places the burden on the grieving parent to reassure you, which is the opposite of what they need.

**Disappearing because it's uncomfortable.** The discomfort of being with a grieving person is real. But their discomfort — their grief — is vastly greater. Stay, even if you don't know what to say.

## What to Say When You Don't Know What to Say

Some honest options:

- "I'm so sorry. I love you and I'm here."
- "I've been thinking about [name] a lot."
- "I don't know what to say, but I didn't want to say nothing."
- "Can I come over?" (And then actually come over.)
- "Is there anything you need right now?" (And accept the answer, whatever it is.)

You do not need to have the right words. You need to be willing to be present and uncomfortable for someone you love.

## For the Long Term

Grief in the years following the loss is often the loneliest period. The immediate community of support has contracted. Life for others has resumed. But for the bereaved parent, it has not resumed — it has restructured itself around an absence.

The most meaningful support in the long term is remembering. Saying the child's name. Sending a message on difficult dates. Asking "how are you doing with all of it?" and meaning it — meaning: *I know this is ongoing, and I haven't forgotten*.

You will not always get it right. No one does. What matters is the willingness to keep showing up.
