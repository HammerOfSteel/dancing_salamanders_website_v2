---

## Excerpt

The bed was too big for a man and a girl, but they filled it anyway.

Tima had colonised the centre — the way she always did, the way small children claim territory without negotiation, their bodies spreading outward from wherever they land until the available surface is theirs and the adults are pressed to the edges like footnotes. She lay diagonal across the duvet, one leg thrown out at an angle that defied the structural limitations of a two-and-a-half-year-old's hip joint, her dark curls fanned across the pillow in a pattern that looked, if you squinted, like a weather system seen from above. Hoppy was face-down beside her. He'd had a long day.

Papa lay on his side at the edge, watching her.

---

The forest drive began at the end of the road. Not metaphorically — literally at the end, past the last cottage, past the trees that separated the houses from the hillside, past the gate that marked the boundary between the lived-in world and the other world, the forest world.

Peter was in front. He was always in front — not because he was fastest but because he navigated the way he did everything, with the body's full commitment. Eira held Tima's hand, as always. The engine; the cargo.

"Look," Eira said, pointing at a mushroom on the verge. "A fairy house."

"It's a mushroom," Papa said.

"It's a fairy house shaped like a mushroom. Mummy says all the mushrooms in the forest are fairy houses."

Peter, without turning: "Your mother would know."
